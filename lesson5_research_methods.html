📐 What makes a research question TESTABLE?
A good research question has all four of these:
1

Specific

It names exactly what is being tested — not vague terms like "music" or "stress" without context.

2

Measurable

The outcome can be counted, scored, or recorded — e.g. "number of words recalled," "reaction time in milliseconds."

3

One clear variable changed

There is one thing that changes (the IV) and one thing that measures the effect (the DV).

4

Answerable by an experiment

You can actually run a study to answer it — not a philosophical or moral question.

📋 The Four Steps in Experimental Design
Every psychological experiment follows these steps. Click each one to expand the detail.
1
Formulate a Research Question
▼

A research question asks what you want to find out. It must be specific, measurable, and focused on one variable that you will change.

"Does listening to music while studying affect memory recall?"

Why it works: It names the IV (music), the DV (memory recall), and the population (students studying). It is specific enough to test.

2
Develop a Hypothesis
▼

A hypothesis is a prediction about what you think will happen — and why. Use this format:

"If [IV], then [DV outcome] because [reason based on prior knowledge]."

Example: "If students listen to music while studying, then their memory recall will decrease, because music divides their attention between the lyrics and the material they are learning."

The "because" is important — it shows you are making a prediction based on reasoning, not just a guess.

3
Identify Variables
▼

Every experiment has three types of variables. For the music and memory experiment:

IV — Independent Presence of music — music vs. silence. This is what we change.

DV — Dependent Number of words recalled from a list. This is what we measure.

CV — Controlled Same word list, same study time, same environment, same instructions for all participants. These are kept the same to make the test fair.

4
Plan the Procedure
▼

The procedure describes exactly how you would run the experiment — step by step — so that someone else could replicate it.

For the music experiment, the procedure might include:

  • Recruit 30 student participants (15 per group)
  • Give all participants the same list of 20 words to study for 5 minutes
  • Group A studies with music; Group B studies in silence
  • After 5 minutes, remove the list and ask all participants to write down as many words as they remember in 2 minutes
  • Count and record the number of correct words recalled per participant

📱 Phone use before bed & morning reaction time

▼
"We investigated whether using a phone before bed affects reaction time in the morning. Our hypothesis is: if students use their phone for one hour before bed, then their reaction time in the morning will be slower, because blue light from screens reduces melatonin production, which disrupts sleep quality and cognitive alertness the next day. Our IV is phone use (one hour of phone use vs. no phone use before bed), our DV is reaction time measured in milliseconds using an online test, and we would control the time of day testing happens and the same reaction time task for everyone. One limitation is that we can't fully control what participants do during the night beyond the phone rule."

🍫 Chocolate before an exam & test scores

▼
"We investigated whether eating chocolate before an exam improves test scores. Our hypothesis is: if students eat dark chocolate 30 minutes before a test, then their scores will be higher, because dark chocolate contains flavonoids that increase blood flow to the brain and may improve short-term cognitive performance. Our IV is chocolate consumption (ate chocolate vs. did not eat chocolate), our DV is score on a 20-question memory test, and we would control the type and amount of chocolate, the test used, and the time between eating and testing. A limitation is that we couldn't control what else participants ate that day, which could affect results."

🚶 Being late & walking speed

▼
"We investigated whether people walk faster when they believe they are late. Our hypothesis is: if people are told they are running 5 minutes late to an appointment, then they will walk measurably faster than people who are told they have plenty of time, because urgency activates the body's stress response, which increases physical output. Our IV is perceived time pressure (told they are late vs. told they have 10 minutes to spare), our DV is walking speed in metres per second over a 20-metre corridor, and we would control the corridor used, the distance, and whether participants are aware they are being timed. A limitation is the ethical challenge of deceiving participants about being late, which would need a thorough debrief."
