📊 Choosing the Right Graph Type
Different data needs different graphs. Click each type to see when to use it and why.
📊
Bar Graph
Compares values between distinct categories (e.g. silence vs music group averages).
✓ Use this for our memory recall data
📈
Line Graph
Shows how a value changes over time (e.g. tracking social media usage year by year).
✓ Use when time is on the x-axis
🥧
Pie Chart
Shows proportions of a whole (e.g. percentage of participants in each condition).
⚠ Avoid for comparing group scores
🏷️ The Four Rules of a Good Graph
Every graph you create must have all four. Click each one to expand the detail.
📌
Rule 1 — Descriptive Title
▼

Your title must describe what is being compared. It should mention the IV and DV.

❌ Bad: "Memory Results"
✓ Good: "Effect of Background Music on the Number of Words Recalled"

A reader should understand the experiment just by reading the title — without seeing any other context.

📏
Rule 2 — Labelled Axes
▼

X-axis (horizontal): always the Independent Variable — what was changed (e.g. Condition: Silence or Music).

Y-axis (vertical): always the Dependent Variable — what was measured (e.g. Number of Words Recalled).

Include units where relevant (e.g. "Words Recalled (out of 10)" or "Time (seconds)").

📐
Rule 3 — Appropriate Scale
▼

Your y-axis scale must start at 0 (unless there is a very good reason not to) and go slightly above your highest data point.

Example: If your highest score is 12, scale from 0 to 15 in steps of 3. Don't use 0–100 for data that only reaches 12 — it makes differences look tiny.

Keep intervals consistent — if you use steps of 2, use 2 all the way up.

🔍
Rule 4 — Identifying Trends and Anomalies
▼

Trend: a general pattern across your data. Example: "Participants in the silence condition recalled more words on average than those in the music condition."

Anomaly: a data point that doesn't fit the trend. Example: "Participant 4 in the music condition recalled 9 words — more than the silence group average — which is unexpected."

Anomalies are not "mistakes" — they may reveal something interesting about individual differences, or they may indicate a data collection error from Lesson 6.

✍️ Step 2 — Written Analysis
Use your graph to write your findings. Cover all three boxes.

📈 Trend

What overall pattern does your graph show? Which group recalled more words on average?

⚠️ Anomaly

Is there any data point that doesn't fit the trend? What might explain it?

🧠 Conclusion

Does your data support your hypothesis from Lesson 5? Explain why or why not.
