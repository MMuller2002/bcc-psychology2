Question 1 of 8 — LTP mechanism

When neurons fire together repeatedly, what is the FIRST thing that happens inside the post-synaptic neuron?

A. New dendritic spines begin to grow B. Calcium ions flow into the post-synaptic neuron C. The myelin sheath thickens around the axon D. Receptor sites on the pre-synaptic neuron increase
Repeated activation causes NMDA receptors to open, allowing calcium influx into the post-synaptic neuron. This calcium signal then triggers the changes that strengthen the connection. Dendritic spine growth comes later — it is the structural outcome of LTP, not the trigger.

Question 2 of 8 — LTP and Unit 1 link

Hebb’s rule states: “neurons that fire together, wire together.” Which Unit 1 concept does this most directly explain?

A. Pruning of unused synaptic connections B. The role of the hippocampus in episodic memory C. Sprouting — the growth of new dendritic spines D. Myelination of the corpus callosum
LTP produces sprouting — the structural growth of new dendritic spines that you labelled in Unit 1. LTP is the electrochemical trigger; sprouting is the physical outcome. The two concepts describe the same process at different levels of analysis.

Question 3 of 8 — Memory consolidation

Why are memories described as “fragile” immediately after learning?

A. The hippocampus hasn’t yet formed a connection with the cortex B. LTP hasn’t occurred yet — neurons haven’t fired together C. The memory exists only as a temporary LTP trace that hasn’t been consolidated into stable LTM D. The sensory register hasn’t transferred the information to STM
After learning, the memory exists as a temporary LTP trace — a change in synaptic sensitivity that can be disrupted. Consolidation (which happens primarily during sleep) stabilises this trace into durable LTM. Without consolidation, even well-encoded memories can fade.

Question 4 of 8 — Sleep stages

Which sleep stage is MOST associated with transferring new memories from the hippocampus to the cortex?

A. REM sleep B. NREM slow-wave sleep C. Light NREM sleep (Stage 1–2) D. The hypnagogic state (falling asleep)
During NREM slow-wave sleep, the hippocampus replays newly encoded information and transfers it to the cortex for long-term storage. REM sleep plays a different role — integrating new memories with existing knowledge networks and consolidating emotional memories.

Question 5 of 8 — REM sleep function

What is the PRIMARY memory function of REM sleep?

A. Replaying hippocampal memories and transferring them to the cortex B. Repairing myelin sheaths damaged during the day C. Integrating new memories with existing knowledge networks D. Clearing waste products from the cerebrospinal fluid
REM sleep is responsible for integrating new memories with existing knowledge and consolidating emotional memories involving the amygdala. Hippocampal replay and cortical transfer is primarily a NREM slow-wave function. (Clearing waste products via the glymphatic system is also a sleep function, but not a memory-specific one.)

Question 6 of 8 — Jamara vs Priya

Jamara studies for 3 hours then sleeps 5 hours. Priya studies for 2 hours then sleeps 8 hours. Who is likely to perform better on a memory test the next day, and why?

A. Jamara, because she spent more time studying and therefore encoded more B. Priya, because 8 hours of sleep provides enough NREM cycles to complete hippocampal consolidation C. They will perform the same, because the total time (study + sleep) is equal D. Jamara, because sleep only helps retention after the first 6 hours
Priya almost certainly performs better. Jamara’s 5 hours of sleep is insufficient for complete NREM hippocampal replay — the fragile LTP traces she formed during study never fully transfer to cortical LTM. Priya’s 8 hours provides two full NREM cycles and adequate REM, completing both the transfer and integration stages. More study time doesn’t compensate for insufficient consolidation.

Question 7 of 8 — Sleep deprivation mechanism

Sleep deprivation impairs memory primarily by disrupting which process?

A. Encoding — preventing LTP from forming during learning B. Retrieval — making it harder to access stored memories C. Consolidation — preventing fragile LTP traces from stabilising into durable LTM D. Attention — reducing the amount of information that reaches the sensory register
Sleep deprivation does not prevent LTP during learning — you can still encode information while sleep-deprived. What it prevents is consolidation: the hippocampal replay and cortical transfer that stabilises fragile traces into durable LTM. The knowledge is encoded but not stabilised, so it fades.

Question 8 of 8 — Application

A student pulls an all-nighter before their Psychology exam, studying until 6am and sleeping only 1 hour before the test. Using today’s content, predict what will happen to their ability to recall the information they studied — and why.

A. They will recall well, because the information was recently encoded and is still in STM B. They will recall poorly, because they didn’t have enough NREM sleep for hippocampal consolidation to complete C. They will recall well, because staying awake keeps LTP active in the hippocampus D. They will recall poorly, because sleep deprivation prevents LTP from forming during study
The student will likely recall poorly. With only 1 hour of sleep, there is virtually no NREM slow-wave sleep — the hippocampus cannot replay and transfer the newly encoded material to the cortex. The LTP traces formed during all-night studying remain fragile and accessible only briefly. This is why “distributed study + full sleep” consistently outperforms “massed study + no sleep”: it’s not how much you study, it’s whether your brain gets the consolidation window it needs.
